History

Sofia is one of the oldest cities in Europe, and remnants of the city’s millenary history can still be seen today alongside modern landmarks.

  • VI-V millennium BCE

    Prehistoric Settlements

    One of these settlements can still be seen in the Sofia History Museum. Prehistoric people decided to settle in today's territory of Sofia attracted by its hot mineral springs.

  • 29 BCE

    Romans Rule

    Around 29 BCE, Sofia became part of the Roman Empire. During this time, the city expanded and several buildings were erected, such as public baths, basilicas, and amphitheaters. Under the Emperor Trajan, the city flourished and became a significant political and economical centre. Sofia reached its greatest height with the establishment of the Council of Serdica, an ecclesiastical meeting of Christian bishops. Following the division of the Roman Empire, the city passed to Byzantium. In the 5th century, the city was destroyed by the Huns, and then rebuilt by the Emperor Justinian who renamed the city to 'Triaditsa'.

  • 809 CE

    Bulgarian and Byzantine Empire

    During the reign of Khan Krum, Sofia was incorporated into the First Bulgarian Empire and changed name to Sredets. The city was under the Bysantine rule from 1018 until 1185, when it was reincorporated into the Bulgarian Empire under the reign of Tsar Ivan Asen I.

  • 1382-1878

    Ottomans Rule

    During the Ottoman-Bulgarian War, the city fell to the Turks. Under the Ottomans, mosques and its minarets dominated the skyline giving the city a distinctive Oriental appearance.
